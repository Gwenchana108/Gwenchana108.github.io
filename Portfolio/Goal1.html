Goal 1: The Botanical Force


Botanical Force is a game that allows learners to explore the game environment, while evaluating and collecting resources to cultivate plant growth and selectively propagate plants with desirable characteristics to produce up to 3 new hybrid breeds.



My Role in Development

Co-designing Storyline and Level Development

Designing and Creating Characters and Artwork

Programming in Construct 3


Objectives




To accomplish this first objective, we began by investigating and playing other games that were programmed in Construct 3, which was the game engine we would be using, so we would have an idea of what Construct 3 would allow you to do as a programmer. We both watched a lot of videos on Construct 3 and would compare notes of the strengths and weaknesses of the engine.


We also found games that were similar to the core dynamic of our game in that they were also games where collecting and exploring were part of what drove the storyline of the game. In having a game that is geared toward collecting and exploring, the game concept began to move to a constructionist view, where we would be having the learner do tasks that would allow them to build their own knowledge base. There was some direct instruction in the game but our plan was to have that become less and less as the learner progressed through the levels.



Secondly, it was very important to both of us to design a game that would provide a meaningful learning experience and also as an added benefit, but fun to play!


As we continued to develop our concept, I began to work on character development. I drew side and front views and added arms in simple poses so that she could be animated in the game.


Christina and I focused on coming up with a solid core loop that would be the understructure of our game and help us to better develop a learning environment for the player that was both fun and challenging.


Our general idea for the core loop was that the player would explore the environment and collect resources while combatting and overcoming different barriers that could interrupt their success with propagating new plant species.

To make sure that actual learning would occur, at this stage we also began thinking about how to assess learning. The first we looked at was making use of scaffolding assessments to provide in-game help and audiovisual clues when the player feels stuck and is not sure what actions to perform in order to deploy the right actions in order for successful propagation. We also decided to use the idea of interweaving hidden/mystery objects to motivate the player to be creative and take risks with their explorations and analysis.



And finally, good project management was an integral part. It was not enough to have a strong idea, we also had to figure out our time frames and create a realistic schedule that we could both adhere to.


I programmed the game, but whenever I would get stuck or find something that was not working, Christina and I would do the research on ways to get around the problem. It was very helpful to have a partner in this aspect. Sometimes in our discussions, we would come up with another solution, just by talking out different scenarios.


Once the character(s) were developed and finalized, I started working on the environment/world of the game. For each level, there will be a different environment that will gradually become more and more challenging.








Reflections



I believe all told we had about 30 days or so from start to finish. I remember at first being both excited and a bit daunted by such a challenge. But it really helped to work with a partner on this project. We both brought just the right amount of excitement, at the right times, to encourage and cheer each other onward!


This is one of my favorite projects from grad school. It had so many moving parts and items to track. It was pleasantly challenging.
